ou’re thinking in exactly the right direction.
For this type of game, the mini-games are not the product — the meta-progression + social competition is the product.

If players only “play one round and leave,” it usually means:

no short-term goal,
no medium-term progression,
no social pressure/rivalry,
no reason to return tomorrow.
What works in games like this (common trends)
1) Multi-layer progression (most important)
Players stay when there are 3 time horizons:

Session goal (5-10 min): “Finish today’s mission”, “Beat my best in this game”
Daily goal (1 day): streaks, daily quests, daily chest
Season goal (1-4 weeks): rank climb, house war, season rewards
If you only have one layer (play game -> get points), churn is high.

2) Social competition > pure score grinding
People return for:

rivalry (“I’m 2 places behind X”),
contribution (“my house needs 500 GP to overtake”),
recognition (“MVP of the day”, “Top improver”).
Leaderboards alone are not enough; they need context + closeness.

3) Controlled novelty cadence
You don’t need many games; you need rotating goals:

weekly featured game,
rotating modifiers (“double CT in Game X today”),
weekend tournaments,
challenge-of-the-day.
This prevents “same thing every day” fatigue.

4) Reward design that feels fair
Reward systems fail when either:

rewards are too flat (no excitement),
or too spiky/pay-to-win (people quit).
Good model:

predictable base rewards,
small bonus rewards for skill/improvement,
periodic milestone payouts.
“Level-by-level” engagement model for HouseWars
Use this exact engagement ladder:

Level 1 — Foundation (must-have retention)
Daily missions (3 per day)
7-day streak with escalating CT
Weekly house race with visible countdown
Personal best + “you improved by X%”
Goal: player has reason to return tomorrow.

Level 2 — Competitive loop
Rival system (“nearest 3 players above/below you”)
House contribution meter (“your GP this week for house”)
Challenge leaderboard clearly visible in challenges page
Weekly MVP badges (cosmetic/title, not power)
Goal: player cares about beating someone, not just collecting points.

Level 3 — Mastery loop
Game-specific mastery tracks (e.g., Speed Tap Tier 1-10)
Milestones: first 1k GP, 10 challenge wins, top 10 finish, etc.
Improvement rewards (bonus for setting new personal best)
Goal: skilled players keep grinding for mastery.

Level 4 — Event loop (re-engagement)
Weekend event mode
Limited-time house missions
“Comeback bonuses” for inactive users (light touch)
Goal: revive dormant players.

Incentive types that work best here
Status incentives: titles, badges, “House Hero” role, featured player
Progress incentives: levels, mastery bars, unlock tracks
Economic incentives: CT boosts, mission reward multipliers
Social incentives: house chat/feed, rivalry card, team milestones
Scarcity incentives: weekly resets, season-exclusive cosmetics
Avoid over-relying on just CT/GP numbers.

Practical anti-boredom formula
For each week, ensure:

1 new reason to log in
1 new reason to compete
1 new reason to improve
1 new reason to care about house outcome
If any one of those is missing, engagement drops.

Metrics to track (to know if this is working)
D1 / D7 retention
Avg sessions per active user/day
Challenge participation rate
% users completing at least 1 daily mission
% users with 3+ day streak
Weekly active users contributing to house GP
If these rise, your progression system is working.

Short answer: no — not all at launch.

For a project like HouseWars, shipping everything at once usually slows you down and makes balancing messy.
Better approach: launch with a focused core loop, then add engagement layers in stages.

What should be in launch (must-have)
Smooth auth/onboarding
Reliable game session scoring (GP/CT)
One clear competitive loop (weekly house race)
Basic challenges (create/join/submit + leaderboard)
Personal progress visibility (rank, best GP, streak/missions or season race — at least one)
What should come right after launch (2-6 weeks)
Daily missions + streaks
Better challenge variety
Rival/friends pressure (duels, near-rank rivals)
Reward cosmetics/badges
Season-end recap + rewards
Why this is better
You validate retention drivers with real users
You can tune economy (GP/CT) before adding more sinks/sources
Less risk of a bloated launch that feels confusing
So yes, those systems are important — but as a roadmap around launch, not necessarily all in v1 launch day.
